# Commonplace — Brand Identity

**Version:** 0.1 (draft)
**Date:** 2026-04-15

This document is the brand's working identity. It captures name rationale, positioning, tone, visual direction, and usage guardrails. It exists to make all downstream decisions (site copy, offer 1-pagers, LinkedIn posts, essays, deck templates) easier and more consistent.

---

## 1. Name and rationale

**Commonplace** — from the Latin *locus communis*, "common topic."

A commonplace book is a personal notebook in which a scholar, scientist, statesman, or writer collects excerpts, observations, frameworks, and ideas from many sources, organised by topic so they can be retrieved, connected, and composed from. The practice dates to antiquity and peaked between the Renaissance and the 19th century — the information-integration technology of the educated mind before the digital era.

The name does three things:

- **States the method.** What the consultancy offers is accumulated, integrated, cross-domain judgment — a commonplace brought to bear.
- **Encodes time.** A commonplace is by definition built over years. The name embeds the 20-year arc so it doesn't need constant justification.
- **Claims AI-era territory.** AI gives access to information. A commonplace gives judgment across time. The brand quietly stakes a claim AI can't easily occupy — a specific human mind, curating and connecting, across decades.

### Why not something shorter / cleaner

Considered: Folio, Metis, Compound, Marginalia, Atlas, Tessera. Each was either generic (Folio), obscure (Metis, Tessera), already heavily used (Atlas), or carried no native story (Compound). Commonplace tells the story on its own.

### Disambiguation

`Commonplace.is` is a UK civic-tech consultation platform (place-based public engagement). Different market, different audience, different TLD. Not a naming competitor.

## 2. Positioning

### Tagline (primary)

> **Applied integration.**

### Tagline (supporting)

> *Twenty years of observation across tech, leadership and organisation design — brought to the problem you're sitting with.*

### One-sentence elevator

> *Commonplace Ventures is a working studio that helps senior leaders design organisations where intelligence — human and machine — flows.*

### Three-sentence version

> *Most consulting today is applied frameworks. Commonplace Ventures is applied integration — a working partnership with senior leaders who are redesigning their organisations for an era in which AI makes new shapes possible. The work starts with rigorous diagnosis of where change is stuck, and proceeds by helping leaders see and build the organisation that wants to emerge.*

### What it is not

- Not an AI strategy boutique
- Not a transformation-programme vendor
- Not a framework library
- Not a deck-and-workshop shop
- Not a generalist management consultancy

### What makes it different

1. **Diagnostic craft** — behaviour-change rigour (COM-B / BCW / BCT) applied to org design. Most advisors assert; this one diagnoses.
2. **Accumulated judgment, not purchased frameworks** — the observation base is decades, not last quarter's book.
3. **Writing as the evidence** — the essays are the portfolio. No case-studies-on-demand.
4. **One voice** — solo-founded, deliberately. Leaders engage with a person, not a pitch team.

## 3. Audience

**Primary:** Senior operators in mid-to-large companies (£100M-£10B rev) who are actively redesigning parts of their organisation for AI-era work.
- Roles: CEO, COO, CTPO, CTO, Head of Product Engineering, Transformation Director, CIO
- Moment: have moved past "should we use AI" — are now sitting with "what does our organisation have to become"
- Pain: transformation programmes that stall, shipping AI features but orgs that can't absorb them, the coordination tax

**Secondary:** Venture-backed scaleups (Series B+) whose next phase of growth requires a real organisation design pass.

**Tertiary:** Senior leaders in transition (exit from corporate → advisory / fractional / founder) who want a sparring partner.

## 4. Tone of voice

### Voice attributes

| More of | Less of |
|---|---|
| Considered | Performative |
| Specific | Abstract |
| Confident | Assertive |
| Plainspoken | Jargon-heavy |
| Historical register occasionally | Pop-business register |
| Declarative sentences | Qualifier-laden hedging |
| Drawing on multiple domains | Riding one framework |
| Quiet authority | Loud confidence |

### Things we do

- Name the specific problem, not the general territory.
- Lead with observations, not conclusions.
- Use historical / literary reference sparingly — it should land, not pose.
- Leave room for the reader's judgment. Don't over-close.
- Pair the concrete with the philosophical. Neither alone is enough.

### Things we don't do

- No emojis (except rare, precise, never decorative).
- No exclamation marks.
- No "hustle" / "grindset" register.
- No LinkedIn-guru syntax: one-line paragraphs stacked for dopamine.
- No "10x", "unleash", "game-changer", "revolutionary".
- No case-study bragging. The writing is the evidence.
- No decks unless requested. The primary artefacts are documents.

### Writing sample (for calibration)

> *Most organisations are designed as if the marginal cost of coordination is high. In 1995 it was. In 2026, increasingly, it isn't — and the shapes that were sensible then are becoming liabilities now. The question for senior leaders this decade is not whether to adopt AI. It's whether the organisation around the AI is the one that can actually use it.*

## 5. Visual direction

### Principle

The visual language is **restrained, classical-literary, digital-modern** — echoing the commonplace book tradition without costume. Think: a contemporary publisher of serious essays, not an agency, not a tech startup.

### Typography

- **Primary (wordmark / headings):** a thoughtful serif. Candidates: *Tiempos Headline*, *GT Sectra*, *Canela*, *Le Monde Livre*. Open-source alternatives: *Crimson Pro*, *EB Garamond*, *Libre Caslon Text*.
- **Secondary (body):** clean, highly readable sans or modern serif. *Inter*, *Söhne*, *Lyon Text*, *Tiempos Text*.
- **Accent (rare, for pull-quotes):** italic from the serif family.

### Colour

Deliberately small palette. Two anchors + one accent + paper.

- **Ink** — `#1A1A1A` — text, wordmark on light
- **Paper** — `#F5F1E8` (warm cream) or `#FAFAF7` — background on marketing surfaces
- **Deep blue** — `#0B2545` or `#132A43` — secondary, for headers and accents
- **One warm accent** — tbd: *Terracotta* (`#B5533C`) or *Oxblood* (`#800020`) or *Ochre* (`#C68A3A`)

### Wordmark direction

Plain. Serif. All lowercase or small caps. Wide letter-spacing. No icon, no symbol initially — the word does the work. If a mark is added later, it's a subtle device from the tradition: an asterism (⁂), a fleuron (❦), a section marker (§), or a stylised paragraph mark (¶).

### Layout

- Generous white space.
- Single-column, long-measure reading layouts.
- Drop caps on essay openings.
- Footnote-style asides in margins or as numbered notes.
- Nothing moves unless it earns it.

### What to avoid

- Gradients.
- Stock photos.
- Isometric illustrations.
- "Tech startup" aesthetic (bold sans, bright colours, flat icons).
- Consultancy-boilerplate aesthetic (blue-and-grey, aspirational abstract photography).

## 7. Usage quickref

**Correct written form:**
- *Commonplace* on first mention in essay, *Commonplace* thereafter.
- Legal: *Commonplace Ventures Ltd*.
- Never "the Commonplace" — always bare.
- Never "CV" or any abbreviation in public surfaces.

**Correct spoken form:**
- *"I'm Ritin from Commonplace."*
- *"This is a piece from Commonplace."*
- *"Commonplace helps senior leaders…"*

**Correct tagline form:**
- Primary: *Applied integration.*
- Never: *"applied integration™"* (no TM theatre).
- Never: a verb version ("Integrating the applied…"). It's a noun phrase and stays one.

## 8. What this identity is for

Every decision — what essay to write next, what to put on the homepage, what to wear to a pitch, what font the offer 1-pager uses, whether to accept a speaking gig, whether to write a thread — should be answerable with: *"Does this fit Commonplace, as defined above?"*

If no — change the thing, not the brand.
