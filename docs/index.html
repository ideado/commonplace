What is a commonplace book?

Commonplace Book — observations, patterns, questions
Org design When teams own outcomes end-to-end, coordination tax drops by half. The org structure is the architecture.
Behaviour science Most "resistance to change" is not resistance — it's a capability gap dressed up as reluctance. COM-B: check opportunity before blaming motivation.
AI in orgs Organisations bolting AI onto a 1995-shaped structure get 1995-shaped results. The shape has to change first.
Platform thinking Platforms built before there are products to run on them become expensive shelfware. The product teaches the platform what to be.
→ These entries connect. That's the point. A commonplace book is how a mind turns exposure into judgment.

Scholars, scientists and writers have kept commonplace books since antiquity — personal notebooks collecting observations from many domains, organised so they can be retrieved, connected, and composed from. Montaigne kept one. Darwin kept one. This is ours.

Writing

The writing comes from the work. Each piece starts as a pattern noticed in the field — something that keeps appearing across different organisations, different decades, different contexts. The essay is the attempt to name it precisely enough to be useful.

About

The Agile Manifesto was written in February 2001 by seventeen practitioners who had spent years in the field, watching what worked and what didn't. They didn't start with a theory. They started with observations — from doing the work and helping others do it.

Commonplace Ventures is in that tradition. Twenty-five years of observation across software delivery, product development, organisation design, and now the AI era — collected, connected, and made available.

Not a framework. Not a methodology. A notebook, shared.

"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value individuals and interactions over processes and tools." — Agile Manifesto, 2001

The same sentence, updated for now: We are uncovering better ways of building organisations by doing it and helping others do it.
