# LinkedIn Draft — AI Broke the Hourly Rate Model

**Status:** Draft
**Date:** 2026-02-25
**Based on:** [[Thomson Reuters - AI Broke the Hourly Rate Model]]

---

KPMG demanded a lower fee from an external audit firm because they knew the firm was using AI to work faster.

At the same time, KPMG charges its own clients a premium — arguing that AI makes each hour more valuable.

Same logic. Both directions. This is the professional services pricing crisis in one anecdote.

Thomson Reuters just published 22 pages of data from 180+ law firms confirming what many of us already sense: hourly billing is structurally incompatible with AI.

The numbers are striking:

- 90% of legal spend still flows through hourly rates — a model unchanged since the 1950s
- Tech spending up 9.7% YoY, talent costs up 10% — firms added AI on top of people, not instead of them
- 52% of corporate leaders plan to move more work in-house over the next 5 years
- Demand contraction forecast from Q1 2026

The core tension is simple. In most businesses, efficiency improves margins. In hourly billing, efficiency cuts revenue. The faster you deliver, the less you earn.

This worked when human limits capped how fast work could get done. AI removed that ceiling.

Now clients can see it. A $200/month AI tool drafts contracts, triages NDAs, and builds templates. Clients no longer need to pay a $40,000 bill to access $4,000 worth of judgement. They bring routine work in-house and hire firms only for what firms are actually good at: navigating grey areas, standing before regulators, and transferring risk.

The report is about legal, but the pattern applies to any professional service that sells time — consulting, accounting, advisory.

The fork is clear: charge for the value you create, or watch your clients replace the hours you used to bill.
